HUMAN PRACTICES

Instituto Nacional de Salud Pública

In order to learn more about antibiotic resistance in our country, we contacted the National Institute of Public Health of Mexico. Thanks to the institute, we were able to talk with the members of the research line in medicines in public health about the access and usage of antibiotics and antimicrobial resistance.

Anahí Dreser

On May 16, a call was made with Anahi Dreser, a researcher specialized in antibiotic usage, health policies and quality of care of the National Institute of Public Health. With this call we were able to know a little more about the current situation of antibiotic resistance in Mexico and analyze alternatives to generate an impact on society. She told us that the magnitude of the problem itself is unknown. This can be associated to the lack of license in the collection of information by governmental entities.

In addition, there is a record that Mexican doctors tend to misuse antibiotics and that patients do not follow their prescribed treatment. We could also discuss the impact that a device like the one we propose and the human practices we plan will have. Dr. Dreser talked to us about the importance of creating a culture of antibiotics, and its importance in modern day.

"Just as the new generations are growing with a culture of recycling, it would be good that they could grow with a culture of the correct use of antibiotics".

Thanks to the information provided, we began to investigate the legislative situation of this issue, analyze the most resistant bacteria in our country and implement E-bug dynamics with patients more vulnerable to infections and with children of various ages.

Jesús Silva Sánchez

We had the opportunity to interview Dr. Jesús Silva Sánchez, leader of the line of research in medicines used in public health to discuss the public policy in the area of ​​health and the existing methods for obtaining new antibiotics.

Asociación Regiomontana de Fibrosis Quística

This association is dedicated to improve the life quality of patients with cystic fibrosis, in the state of Nuevo León, Mexico. They primarily give treatments and medications to patients with low incomes, and to achieve this, they organize fundraising events.

Through this contact we were able to meet members of the association, including children affected by this disease, which helped us to see the reality that patients live.

Due to the relevance of the acquisition of antibiotic resistance, the team focused on the erroneous use of antibiotics, which is one of the main causes of this problem. In Mexico, laws have been created to avoid the purchase of antibiotics without a medical prescription, but these have shown ineffective. In addition, the packages of these medications lack inserts that give consumers sufficient information for its correct use.

For the above, this information was exposed in the Congress of Nuevo León, The approaches with the experts made us see that innovations in the area of ​​health in our country may become obsolete due to the deficiencies of the same health system. In a nutshell, it is no use for finding new antibiotics or creating new medications if people or institutions are going to misuse them.

The inadequate prescription of antibiotics in the Mexican public sector is a serious problem. It has been shown that respiratory and stomach infections are the worst treated. According to Gutierrez et al, "Research carried out mostly during the 1980s and 1990s, concluded that between 60 and 80% of patients with HAIs and EDAS received antibiotics in public and private primary health services in the country, when in fact their use was justified only in 10 to 15% of the cases. "(2008). Additionally, it is known that justified prescriptions are made in the most advanced hospitals. Nevertheless, many of the doses or treatments provided were excessive, which represents a serious threat to patients. In conclusion, most antibiotic prescriptions are unjustified or inadequate and this has negative consequences for society.

In monetary terms, the inappropriate use of antibiotics comes to represent 11% of the public spending in health. This expense is caused by incorrect prescriptions and the lack of follow-up of treatments by patients. In addition, the incorrect prescriptions of medications come to represent 1 or 2 percent more than the patient's daily minimum wage. The bad prescriptions come to represent a large economic spill that affects the Mexican economy.

In Mexico, a large part of the strategies implemented has been in the educational area and directives of the health sector. First, training in prescriptions in medical schools began to improve. This was intended to improve prescriptions for respiratory and stomach infections. In the same way, the use of clinical guidelines for better prescriptions in public health institutes was encouraged. Nevertheless, there is no study that talks about the results of the implementation of these techniques. In a few words, it has sought to improve prescriptions in Mexico, but it is not known if it is working. In order to begin a change in the subject of legislation in the area of ​​health, iGEM Tec Monterrey began to promote an amendment to the Official Mexican Standard NOM-072-SSA1-2012. This rule establishes the guidelines to follow in the labeling of medicines in Mexico, of which we proposed an improvement in the warnings about the damage of antibiotics and the modification of an article to force pharmaceutical companies to improve their labeling and the information provided about your medications. Our strategy was to start with the modification of a rule that directly affects society. With this modification, clearer and more concise information on medicines would be offered to society. This type of modification would lead to the proper use of free medications and antibiotics, as well as to warn in a stronger way the risk involved in the inappropriate use of antibiotics. In addition to the above, it will be a precedent to propose further modifications in the national health sector. Modifications that seek to establish a clearer authority for the health sector, strengthen health regulations and improve the education system of the health sciences area. In order to make our proposal a reality, we took on the task of seeking help in the local congress of the state of Nuevo León (Mexico), where we were able to collaborate with the deputy Rosa Isela Castro who is part of the health commission. Initially, we were able to present our bill to the deputy and her team, with them we were able to discuss the pros and cons of this initiative, in addition to the legal mechanisms to make it happen.
